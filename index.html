Curatorial Statement

This exhibition represents the culmination of a semester's work in the Curating Data elective at Aarhus University. As a collective of four students, we have revisited our individual archives—datasets, reports, and visualizations—to articulate the histories that unfolded in our learning during this course, taught by Midas Nouwens and Magdalena Regina Tyzlik-Carver.

Throughout the semester, our curating data efforts have been shaped by the understanding that data are not neutral facts but constructed artefacts. Guided by Wernimont's notion of quantification as world-making and Kitchin's definition of data as capta, we have approached data practices as interpretive, political, and situated. Working with the Curating Data Model has allowed us to conceptualise our assignments, workshops, and fieldwork within a shared taxonomy of data practices, highlighting how collection, classification, cleaning, and visualisation actively shape meaning.

Our practical work has involved critically observing and performing data practices, identifying the assumptions embedded within them, and contributing to the ongoing expansion of the model. In this way, we have come to see curating data as a critical, reflective, and methodological practice that negotiates theory and technique to reveal how data come into being and how they participate in making worlds.

The Personal Archive

Jakob Munch-Brandt
"Working with my clothes as data taught me that data is never objective. When I actively had to select information categories about my clothes, it turned to capta instead data, just as Kitchin describes: "Data in its Latin root are all the potential data that can be sourced from a phenomenon, and capta are all those data that are actually captured" (Kitchin, 2025, p. 25).

I also realized that my categories as metadata aren't neutral, but reflects my personal viewpoints about the clothes, which points to what Acker talks about here: "Because metadata are created and used by humans, they inherently advance particular worldviews onto the ideas, artifacts, and experiences that are used to describe." (Acker, 2021, p. 1)"

Object-Oriented Data

Sebastian Kjær
"Finally, Rowley's paper about the DIKW hierarchy gives a framework for understanding how my dataset moves from raw measurements to knowledge. According to Rowley, the transformation from data to information and knowledge is nonlinear and depends on context and interpretation (Rowley, 2007). In this dataset, the measurements only become information when I analyze relationships such as loft and length in relation to distance, and they turn into knowledge when I translate them into practical advice on choosing a club when playing."

Categorizing History

Jakob Munch-Brandt
"Translating Joseph Bilé's Dekoloniale biography into Wikidata made it clear that curating data is never a neutral transfer of facts. Following Nadim's notion of datafication and Loukissas' argument that all data are local, I became aware of how ontologies, properties, and platform constraints both enable new connections and quietly erase parts of his story."

— Jakob Munch-Brandt
References

Nadim, T. (2021). The datafication of nature: Data formations and new scales in natural history. Journal of the Royal Anthropological Institute, 27(S1), 62–75.

Loukissas, Y. A. (2019). All data are local: Thinking critically in a data-driven society. MIT Press.

Data Structures

Mikkel Tagesen Knudsen
"By working through these categories, we realized that categorization itself produced new insights; for example, identifying Bilé's connection to other activists revealed the extent of his transnational political involvement. As Bowker and Star note, every act of classification both reveals and conceals aspects of the world; it makes certain narratives legible while obscuring others."

— Mikkel Tagesen Knudsen
References

Bowker, G. C., & Star, S. L. (1999). Sorting Things Out: Classification and Its Consequences. MIT Press.

Visualising and Visceralising

How visualization can feel personal, not only informative

We explored in PowerBI. We designed in Canva. We tried to move from analysis to emotion.

Visualizing Complexity

Jakob Munch-Brandt
"In my first visual analytic I follow Shneiderman's mantra of "overview first, zoom and filter, then details-on-demand" and keep close to Tufte's ideal of a high data-ink ratio. In the second, I shift from analysis to visceralisation, using a mountain metaphor to evoke emotion and show how framing can make depression among high-achieving students feel more or less prevalent."

— Jakob Munch-Brandt
References

Shneiderman, B. (1996). The eyes have it: A task by data type taxonomy for information visualizations. In Proceedings 1996 IEEE Symposium on Visual Languages (pp. 336–343).

Tufte, E. R. (2018). The visual display of quantitative information (2nd ed.). Graphics Press.

Analytical Lenses

Mikkel Tagesen Knudsen
"Instead of focusing on analytical precision, the goal is to visceralise the data, to evoke emotion and provoke reflection on what the dataset does not show. This aligns with feminist data principles that argue for the importance of emotion and embodiment in understanding data (D'Ignazio & Klein, 2020)."

— Mikkel Tagesen Knudsen
