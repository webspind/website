Curatorial Statement

This exhibition represents the culmination of a semester's work in Data Curation at Aarhus University. As a collective of four students, we have revisited our individual archives—datasets, reports, and visualizations—to articulate the histories that unfolded in our learning during this elective course.

Throughout the semester, our curating data efforts have been shaped by the understanding that data are not neutral facts but constructed artefacts. Guided by Wernimont’s notion of quantification as world-making and Kitchin’s definition of data as capta, we have approached data practices as interpretive, political, and situated. Working with the Curating Data Model has allowed us to conceptualise our assignments, workshops, and fieldwork within a shared taxonomy of data practices, highlighting how collection, classification, cleaning, and visualisation actively shape meaning.

Our practical work has involved critically observing and performing data practices, identifying the assumptions embedded within them, and contributing to the ongoing expansion of the model. In this way, we have come to see curating data as a critical, reflective, and methodological practice that negotiates theory and technique to reveal how data come into being and how they participate in making worlds.

The Personal Archive

Assignment 1
"Working with my clothes as data taught me that data is never objective. When I actively had to select information categories about my clothes, it turned to capta instead data, just as Kitchin describes: “Data in its Latin root are all the potential data that can be sourced from a phenomenon, and capta are all those data that are actually captured” (Kitchin, 2025, p. 25).

I also realized that my categories as metadata aren’t neutral, but reflects my personal viewpoints about the clothes, which points to what Acker talks about here: “Because metadata are created and used by humans, they inherently advance particular worldviews onto the ideas, artifacts, and experiences that are used to describe.” (Acker, 2021, p. 1)"
References

Kitchin, R. (2025). Critical data studies: An A to Z guide to concepts and methods. Polity Press.

Acker, A. (2021). Metadata. In N. B. Thylstrup et al. (Eds.), Uncertain archives: Critical keywords for big data (pp. 33-42). MIT Press.

Object-Oriented Data

Assignment 1
"Finally, Rowley’s paper about the DIKW hierarchy gives a framework for understanding how my dataset moves from raw measurements to knowledge. According to Rowley, the transformation from data to information and knowledge is nonlinear and depends on context and interpretation (Rowley, 2007). In this dataset, the measurements only become information when I analyze relationships such as loft and length in relation to distance, and they turn into knowledge when I translate them into practical advice on choosing a club when playing."

— Sebastian Kjær
References

Rowley, J. (2007). The wisdom hierarchy: Representations of the DIKW hierarchy. Journal of Information Science, 33(2), 163–180.
